Miscellaneous Devices
This pages covers how to and why it is necessary to seize other devices which may be found at a crime scene. The devices covered here are:
  • Surveilance Systems
  • External Storage
  • TVs
  • Smart TVs
  • Games Consoles
Section 1 - Surveilance Systems
Regardless of whether the evidence was recorded by a digital or analog system, the investigator should make a note of the make and model of the recording device and all important details about the recording system including; how many cameras are connected and recording, mode of recording (time-lapse, delay, real-time), the current and recorders date and time
Section 1a - Analogue Surveilance System
Analogue systems record to a tape which deteriorates with each play. The first responder must make sure the tape has been stopped before ejecting the tape and removing the write protection tab to prevent it from being recorded over. The tape should then be placed in the appropriate evidence bag and labeled. The responding officer should identify all video or audio evidence that may exist. In addition to surveillance cameras at the scene, other footage may be available nearby
Section 1b - Digital Surveilance System
There are two types of digital surveilance devices; embedded stand-alone and PC-based. Both types generally record the audio and video to hard drives, however, some systems record to secure digital (SD) cards and other removable media. If the footage is on an SD card, see External Storage Devices. If the device saves to a hard drive, follow the process for a computer.
Section 1c - Potential Evidence from Surveilance Systems
Surveilance systems could provide vital footage of a perpetrator approaching or fleeing a scene. Video evidence could also be used to identify any other participants involved in the crime.
Even if the recording does not appear to be very clear or useful, all relevant footage should be collected. The forensic enhancement process carried out in the lab, may recover details that aren’t noticeable when viewing or listening to the unprocessed recording.
Section 2a - External Storage Devices
Memory Sticks are small and lightweight forms of removable data storage with USB connections. These devices are also referred to as flash drives or thumb drives. They are easy to conceal and are often found hidden as a wrist watches, a keychain fob, or any number of common and unique objects. The ability to conceal also makes them easy to transport.
Section 2b - Potential Evidence from External Storage Devices
These devices may contain information such as e-mails, Internet browsing history (including chat logs), photographs, databases, financial records and event logs that can be valuable evidence in an investigation.
Section 3a - Televisions (TVs)
Televisions (TVs) come in a variety of forms but are all recognised by the screen aspect of the product. They can be found in a variety of colours and sizes.
Section 3b - Potential Evidence from TVs
Standard TVs do not have internet access or any storage capacity without additional devices, therefore these would not be required to be seized from a scene. Any additional devices such as smart boxes, firesticks or other internet connected streaming devices connected to the TV should be seized as explained in External Devices.

Section 4b - Potential Evidence from Smart TVs
Smart TVs can contain internet history, app data as well as images or videos.
Section 5a - Games Consoles
General notes for consoles regardless of power state:
  • Label all cables connected to the device and the appropriate connection ports
  • Collect all cables, handbooks and user manuals from the user

If the device is OFF, do NOT turn the device on. Package device and cables in secure bags and label for evidence.

If the device is ON, photograph the device and its surroundings including the screen.
Remove all power suply and tape over all slots and ports on the device.
Package and secure the device. Label with details of the device.
Section 5b - Potential Evidence from Games Consoles
Games consoles can store data in addition to the game data from users activitycould include text, images, audio, video, etc. Internet access, information, Email, Basic personal computing functions
